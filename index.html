Lesson 1What Is a Chemical Bond?
⚛️ Chemical Bond = Electrostatic Attraction That Holds Atoms Together

A chemical bond forms when atoms interact to reach a lower, more stable energy state — typically by achieving a full outer shell of 8 valence electrons (Octet Rule). Atoms do this by either transferring electrons (ionic) or sharing electrons (covalent).

The 2 Main Bond Types

  • Ionic: Metal + Nonmetal → electrons TRANSFERRED
  • Covalent: Nonmetal + Nonmetal → electrons SHARED
  • Metallic: Metal + Metal → electron sea

The Periodic Table Rule

  • Left of the staircase = Metals
  • Right of the staircase = Nonmetals
  • Metal + Nonmetal → Ionic bond
  • Nonmetal + Nonmetal → Covalent bond
🧂 NaCl = Ionic💧 H₂O = Covalent⚗️ NH₃ = Covalent🪨 MgO = Ionic
Regents Concept Atoms bond to achieve noble gas electron configuration (full outer shell). Noble gases don't bond because they're already stable.
Key Rule The quickest bond-type test: Is a metal involved with a nonmetal? → Ionic. Two nonmetals only? → Covalent. It's that simple for most Regents questions!

When we say atoms "want" to bond, we mean bonding lowers their potential energy — it's thermodynamically favorable. The octet rule works because the n=2 shell holds a maximum of 8 electrons (2s² 2p⁶). Hydrogen only needs 2 electrons to fill its 1s shell (matching helium).

A bond is NOT a chemical reaction — the atoms' identities are conserved. Only their electron arrangements change. Think of it like two people holding hands — they're still two separate people, just connected.

The staircase on the periodic table runs from B (boron) through At (astatine). Elements along the staircase are metalloids and can behave as either metals or nonmetals depending on context.

🧂

Salt (NaCl): Sodium (metal) transfers 1 electron to chlorine (nonmetal) → ionic bond. You literally eat this reaction's product every day.

💧

Water (H₂O): Oxygen and hydrogen are both nonmetals → covalent bond. This bond is why water exists as a liquid and supports all life on Earth.

💎

Diamond: Pure carbon — nonmetal bonding to nonmetal covalently in a giant 3D network. Result: hardest natural substance on Earth.

Lesson 2Ionic Bonds — Electron Transfer
🔋 Metal + Nonmetal → Electron TRANSFER → Cation⁺ + Anion⁻

In an ionic bond, a metal loses electrons becoming a positively charged cation, and a nonmetal gains those electrons becoming a negatively charged anion. The electrostatic attraction between the opposite charges IS the ionic bond.

Ionic Compound Properties

  • Hard, brittle crystal lattice

Common Ionic Compounds

Regents Example What type of bond forms in CaCl₂? Ca is a metal (Group 2), Cl is a nonmetal (Group 17) → Ionic bond. Ca loses 2e⁻ → Ca²⁺. Each Cl gains 1e⁻ → Cl⁻. Two Cl⁻ ions needed to balance Ca²⁺.
⚠️ Regents Trap! Ionic compounds conduct electricity ONLY in aqueous solution (aq) or when melted — NOT as a solid! In solid form, ions are locked in the lattice and can't move to carry charge. This is tested every year!

When Na (11p, 11e) loses 1 electron → Na⁺ (11p, 10e). Now it has the same electron configuration as neon (noble gas)! When Cl (17p, 17e) gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ (17p, 18e). Now it has argon's configuration. Both ions achieve noble gas configuration — maximum stability.

Why is the ionic radius of Cl⁻ larger than neutral Cl? Cl⁻ has 18 electrons but still only 17 protons. The nuclear charge hasn't changed, but there are now more electrons to pull apart. Less nuclear pull per electron = larger, more diffuse electron cloud = bigger ionic radius.

In a solid ionic compound, millions of ions pack together in a crystal lattice — each Na⁺ surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions, and each Cl⁻ surrounded by 6 Na⁺ ions. This maximizes attractions and minimizes repulsions, giving ionic compounds their characteristic high melting points.

🚗

Road Salt: CaCl₂ is scattered on icy roads. As an ionic compound, it dissolves readily, releasing ions that lower the freezing point of water below 0°C — melting ice without plowing.

⚡

Electrolytes: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻ dissolved in body fluids conduct electrical signals in nerves and muscles. Your heartbeat is literally powered by ionic movement.

💊

Antacids (MgO, Mg(OH)₂): Magnesium oxide is an ionic compound that reacts with stomach acid (HCl) to neutralize it. It's the active ingredient in milk of magnesia.

Lesson 3Covalent Bonds — Electron Sharing
🔗 Nonmetal + Nonmetal → Electron SHARING → Molecule

In a covalent bond, two nonmetals share one or more pairs of electrons. Neither atom fully gives up electrons — instead, the shared electrons experience attraction to both nuclei simultaneously. The result is a molecule.

The 2 electrons oscillate between both nuclei — attracted to both simultaneously. This IS the covalent bond!

Bond Order & Strength

  • Single bond (–): 1 shared pair (2e⁻)
  • Double bond (=): 2 shared pairs (4e⁻)
  • Triple bond (≡): 3 shared pairs (6e⁻)
  • More bonds = stronger & shorter

Covalent Compound Properties

  • Generally lower MP/BP than ionic
  • Often gas or liquid at room temp
  • Do NOT conduct electricity
  • May dissolve in water (polar ones)
Regents Example What type of bond forms in N₂? N is a nonmetal bonded to another nonmetal → Covalent. Specifically a triple bond (≡), because each N has 3 unpaired valence electrons and they share all 3 pairs (6 electrons total).
Regents Tip Covalent compounds NEVER conduct electricity in any state — there are no free ions or electrons. Unlike ionic compounds, dissolved covalent compounds still don't conduct. This contrast is heavily tested!

In a covalent bond, both atoms "count" the shared electrons toward filling their valence shell. In H₂O, the O atom counts 2 bonding pairs (4e⁻ shared with H atoms) plus 2 lone pairs (4e⁻ unshared) = 8 electrons total. Full octet! Each H counts 2 electrons (the bonding pair) = full duet!

Bond strength increases with bond order: A triple bond (N≡N, 945 kJ/mol) is much harder to break than a double bond (O=O, 498 kJ/mol) or a single bond (H–H, 436 kJ/mol). This is why N₂ gas is so unreactive — its triple bond is extremely strong.

Lone pairs are electrons on an atom NOT involved in bonding. They still count toward the octet but don't form bonds. In NH₃, N has 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair. That lone pair makes NH₃ polar and gives it its pyramidal shape (not covered until next unit).

🌿

CO₂ & Photosynthesis: Carbon dioxide has two C=O double covalent bonds. Plants break these bonds using light energy to rearrange carbon into glucose — every carb you eat started as a covalent bond in CO₂.

💧

Water's covalent O–H bonds: The covalent bonds in a water molecule hold it together, while a different force (hydrogen bonding — an IMF) attracts water molecules to each other. Understanding this difference explains almost everything about water.

🛢️

Gasoline (C₈H₁₈): Octane is a pure covalent molecule. The enormous energy stored in all those C–H and C–C bonds is released as heat when it combusts, powering your car engine.

Lesson 4Lewis Dot Diagrams
🔵 Lewis Dots: Group # = Valence e⁻ = Number of Dots

A Lewis dot diagram shows an atom's valence electrons as dots arranged around the element symbol. The number of dots equals the group number. Dots are placed one per side first (up, right, down, left), then paired up — a maximum of 2 per side.

Select an element to see its Lewis dot diagram

Valence Electrons by Group

  • Group 1 → 1 dot
  • Group 2 → 2 dots
  • Group 13 → 3 dots
  • Group 14 → 4 dots (all unpaired)
  • Group 15 → 5 dots (1 pair + 3 single)
  • Group 16 → 6 dots (2 pairs + 2 single)
  • Group 17 → 7 dots (3 pairs + 1 single)
  • Group 18 → 8 dots (4 pairs — noble gas)

Unpaired Dots = Bonds Formed

  • H (1 dot) → 1 bond
  • C (4 dots) → 4 bonds
  • N (3 unpaired) → 3 bonds
  • O (2 unpaired) → 2 bonds
  • F (1 unpaired) → 1 bond
  • Noble gases → 0 bonds (already full!)

Pattern: Bonds = 8 − Group# (for groups 15–17)

Regents Example Draw the Lewis dot diagram for sulfur (S). S is Group 16 → 6 valence electrons. Place dots: 1 on each side (4 sides = 4e⁻), then 2 more pair up with existing dots. Result: 2 lone pairs + 2 single dots. S typically forms 2 bonds.
Regents Tip For Lewis dot of an ION, adjust the dot count: add 1 dot per negative charge (gained e⁻), remove 1 dot per positive charge (lost e⁻). So Cl⁻ has 8 dots (full octet), Na⁺ has 0 dots.

G.N. Lewis developed these diagrams in 1916 — before quantum mechanics fully explained electron orbitals. They're simplified but remarkably powerful for predicting bonding, molecular shape, and polarity.

Drawing molecular Lewis structures: (1) Count total valence electrons from all atoms. (2) Place the least electronegative atom in the center. (3) Connect atoms with single bonds. (4) Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs to satisfy octets. (5) If atoms need more electrons, convert lone pairs to double/triple bonds.

Practice — NH₃ (ammonia): N has 5 val e⁻, each H has 1. Total = 5 + 3(1) = 8e⁻ = 4 pairs. Three pairs are used for N–H bonds. One pair remains as a lone pair on N. This lone pair is key — it makes NH₃ polar and gives nitrogen a partial negative end.

💊

Drug Design: Pharmaceutical chemists draw Lewis structures to understand where a drug molecule will have partial charges, which determines how it interacts with receptor proteins in your body.

🔋

Batteries: The Lewis structure of lithium (1 dot, 1 bond tendency) explains why Li⁺ ions are perfect for battery chemistry — they readily give up that one electron, creating a flow of charge.

Regents Example Determine bond type in HCl. EN(Cl)=3.0, EN(H)=2.1. END = |3.0−2.1| = 0.9. Since 0.5 ≤ 0.9 ≤ 1.7 → Polar Covalent. Cl end is δ− (more electronegative). H end is δ+.
Regents Tip Always take the absolute value! You can never have a negative END. If both atoms are the same element (like O₂, Cl₂), END = 0 always → nonpolar covalent. No need to even look up the EN values!

Electronegativity increases going across a period from left to right (more protons = stronger nuclear pull on electrons) and up a group (smaller atom = electrons closer to nucleus = stronger pull). Fluorine (top-right corner) is #1. Noble gases have no EN because they don't bond.

In polar covalent bonds, the electron cloud is asymmetric — it shifts toward the more electronegative atom. This creates a permanent dipole (δ+ and δ−). Water's permanent dipole is why it dissolves ionic compounds so well — the δ+ H atoms attract anions; the δ− O atoms attract cations.

The 1.7 cutoff is approximate and somewhat arbitrary — different sources use 1.6 or 2.0. Bond character is truly a continuous spectrum. HF (END=1.9) technically falls in "ionic" by our scale but behaves mostly covalently. For the Regents, always use 1.7 exactly.

🌊

Oil & Water Don't Mix: Water is polar (END ≈ 1.4). Oil molecules are nonpolar (END ≈ 0). Polar molecules are only strongly attracted to other polar molecules — "like dissolves like." This is the molecular reason oil floats on water.

🧲

MRI Machines: MRI works because water's polar bonds make protons respond to magnetic fields. Doctors can image soft tissue because the polar O–H bonds in body water create a detectable magnetic signal.

Lesson 6Naming Ionic Compounds
📛 Ionic = Metal name + Nonmetal root + "-ide" (or polyatomic name)

Ionic compound names tell you exactly which cation and anion are present. Follow these four rules every time:

  1. Name the metal (cation) first, exactly as on the periodic table. Na⁺ = sodium. Ca²⁺ = calcium. Al³⁺ = aluminum. No changes.
  2. Name the nonmetal (anion): root + "-ide". Cl⁻ → chloride. O²⁻ → oxide. S²⁻ → sulfide. N³⁻ → nitride. Br⁻ → bromide.
  3. Transition metals need Roman numerals. They have multiple possible charges. Figure out the charge from the anion(s), then write: iron(II), iron(III), copper(I), copper(II), etc.
  4. Polyatomic ions keep their names — never add "-ide". NO₃⁻ = nitrate. SO₄²⁻ = sulfate. NH₄⁺ = ammonium. CO₃²⁻ = carbonate. OH⁻ = hydroxide. PO₄³⁻ = phosphate.

Formula → Name

  • NaCl → sodium chloride
  • MgO → magnesium oxide
  • FeCl₂ → iron(II) chloride
  • FeCl₃ → iron(III) chloride
  • Cu₂O → copper(I) oxide
  • NH₄NO₃ → ammonium nitrate
  • K₂CO₃ → potassium carbonate

Name → Formula

  • potassium bromide → KBr
  • calcium chloride → CaCl₂
  • iron(III) oxide → Fe₂O₃
  • zinc sulfide → ZnS
  • sodium hydroxide → NaOH
  • ammonium sulfate → (NH₄)₂SO₄
Regents Example Name FeCl₃. Fe is a transition metal. Each Cl = −1; three Cl = −3. Fe must be +3 for neutrality → Fe³⁺ = iron(III). Answer: iron(III) chloride.
Strategy for Transition Metals: ALWAYS find the anion charge first, multiply by subscript, then calculate what charge the metal MUST have so charges sum to zero. The compound is always electrically neutral!

Why do only some metals need Roman numerals? Main-group metals (Groups 1, 2, 13) have only one possible charge: Group 1 = always +1, Group 2 = always +2, Al = always +3. You never need to specify because there's only one option. Transition metals (Groups 3–12) can have multiple charges (e.g., Fe can be +2 or +3; Cu can be +1 or +2; Pb can be +2 or +4), so the Roman numeral is essential information.

Polyatomic ions to memorize: Think of them as "pre-assembled" charged groups that stay intact. NH₄⁺ (ammonium, +1) · OH⁻ (hydroxide, −1) · NO₃⁻ (nitrate, −1) · NO₂⁻ (nitrite, −1) · SO₄²⁻ (sulfate, −2) · SO₃²⁻ (sulfite, −2) · CO₃²⁻ (carbonate, −2) · PO₄³⁻ (phosphate, −3) · HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate, −1).

The IUPAC naming system was established so that every compound has one unambiguous name understood by chemists worldwide. Before IUPAC, "blue vitriol" (CuSO₄·5H₂O) and "oil of vitriol" (H₂SO₄) confused students for centuries.

💊

Iron supplements: "Iron(II) sulfate" (FeSO₄) and "iron(III) chloride" (FeCl₃) are very different compounds — different solubilities, different toxicities, different medical uses. The Roman numeral is clinically important.

🌾

Fertilizers: Ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃) and ammonium phosphate ((NH₄)₃PO₄) are key agricultural fertilizers. The names tell farmers exactly what N and P content they're adding to soil.

Why does CO vs CO₂ matter so enormously? Carbon monoxide (CO) bonds to hemoglobin 200× more tightly than oxygen — it displaces O₂ from your blood, causing suffocation even while your blood looks red. CO₂ is nontoxic at normal concentrations and essential for plant photosynthesis. One atom difference in the name = life-or-death difference in reality.

When NOT to use prefixes: Only for covalent (nonmetal–nonmetal) compounds! Never use prefixes for ionic compounds. You'd never say "disodium monochloride" for Na₂Cl because: (1) that compound doesn't exist in real life, and (2) ionic charges determine the ratio, not a choice.

Naming acids: HCl dissolved in water = hydrochloric acid. HCl as a pure gas = hydrogen chloride. The same compound has a different name depending on state — this is beyond Unit 4 but worth knowing for the Regents.

🚨

CO Detectors: Carbon MONoxide detectors are required by law in homes because CO (not CO₂) is the deadly one. One prefix — mono vs. di — makes the difference between what kills you and what plants breathe.

☀️

Ozone (O₃): High in the atmosphere, ozone (trioxide would be its prefix name) protects Earth from UV. At ground level, it's a pollutant causing respiratory problems. Same formula, totally different impact depending on where it is.

Lesson 8Molecular Polarity & Intermolecular Forces
🧲 Stronger IMFs → Higher BP · Higher MP · Lower Vapor Pressure

Intermolecular forces (IMFs) are attractions between molecules — NOT the bonds within molecules. They determine physical properties: boiling point, melting point, and solubility. Stronger IMF = more energy to separate molecules = higher BP/MP.

🔷 Polar Molecule
  • Has polar bonds AND asymmetric shape
  • Net dipole moment ≠ 0
  • Has δ+ end and δ− end
  • Examples: H₂O (bent), NH₃ (pyramidal), HCl
🔶 Nonpolar Molecule
  • Only nonpolar bonds, OR symmetric shape
  • Dipoles cancel out → net dipole = 0
  • No permanent dipole
  • Examples: O₂, CO₂ (linear), CCl₄ (tetrahedral)

IMFs don't change the compound — they only determine physical state and properties!

London Dispersion Forces

  • Present in ALL molecules (polar and nonpolar)
  • Caused by temporary uneven electron distribution
  • Increase with molecular size (more electrons)
  • Largest molecules have strongest LDF
  • Weakest IMF overall

Hydrogen Bonding

  • Strongest type of IMF
  • H must be bonded directly to F, O, or N
  • Examples: H₂O, HF, NH₃
  • Explains water's unusually high BP (100°C)
  • Critical for DNA double helix structure
Regents Example Rank H₂O, H₂S, and CH₄ in order of increasing boiling point. CH₄ has only LDF (weakest). H₂S has dipole–dipole. H₂O has hydrogen bonding (H directly bonded to O — strongest). Order: CH₄ < H₂S < H₂O ✓
Regents Tip To compare boiling points: (1) Does either have H-bonding? (H bonded to N, O, or F?) If yes, it boils higher. (2) If same IMF type, larger molecule = stronger LDF = higher BP. These two rules answer almost every IMF question!

Why does water have such a uniquely high boiling point? Water's MW is only 18 g/mol — smaller than H₂S (34 g/mol) — yet water boils at 100°C while H₂S boils at −60°C. The entire 160°C difference is due to hydrogen bonding. Each water molecule can form up to 4 H-bonds (2 lone pairs on O can accept; 2 O–H bonds can donate). This cooperative H-bond network requires enormous energy to disrupt.

London Dispersion Forces are more important for large molecules: Noble gases are nonpolar and have only LDF — but He (BP = −269°C) boils much lower than Xe (BP = −108°C). Xenon has 54 electrons creating much larger instantaneous dipoles. This is why I₂ is a solid at room temp while F₂ is a gas — same bond type, very different LDF strength.

CO₂ is linear and nonpolar despite polar C=O bonds: The two C=O dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and cancel out. CO₂ has only LDF, which is why it's a gas at room temperature. Compare to H₂O — similar size, but polar and H-bonding → liquid. Shape determines molecular polarity.

🧊

Why ice floats: When water freezes, H-bonds force molecules into a hexagonal lattice with more empty space than liquid water → ice is less dense. Ice floating insulates liquid water below, allowing fish to survive frozen winters.

🧬

DNA Double Helix: The two strands of DNA are held together by hydrogen bonds (A–T = 2 H-bonds, G–C = 3 H-bonds). Strong enough to maintain structure, but weak enough to "unzip" during DNA replication. Perfect molecular engineering!

🦎

Gecko feet: Geckos cling to walls using London Dispersion Forces between millions of microscopic toe hairs (setae) and the wall surface. A purely quantum mechanical, IMF-powered superpower.

Lesson 9Metallic Bonding & Network Covalent Solids
🔩 Metal + Metal → Electron Sea · Metal atoms release valence e⁻ into a shared "sea"

A metallic bond forms between metal atoms. Each metal atom releases its valence electrons into a delocalized "electron sea" that surrounds all the positive metal ions (cations). The attraction between the mobile electrons and the fixed cations IS the metallic bond.

Properties Explained by Electron Sea

  • Conducts electricity & heat: Free electrons carry charge and energy in any direction
  • Malleable & ductile: Layers of cations slide without breaking — electrons re-surround them
  • Lustrous (shiny): Free electrons absorb and re-emit light
  • High melting point: Strong attraction between cations and electron sea

Metallic Bond vs. Other Bonds

  • Ionic: electrons transferred → fixed ions in lattice
  • Covalent: electrons shared between specific atoms
  • Metallic: electrons delocalized — belong to ALL atoms simultaneously
  • Metal + Metal → Metallic bond
  • Metal + Nonmetal → Ionic bond
Regents Tip "Which substance conducts electricity as a solid?" → The answer is always a metal (metallic bonding = free electrons as solid). Ionic compounds only conduct as liquid/aqueous. Covalent compounds never conduct.

🔮 Network Covalent Solids — The Exception

Most covalent compounds have low melting points (weak IMFs between molecules). But network covalent solids are a special case — they consist of an enormous 3D network of covalent bonds extending throughout the entire crystal. There are no separate molecules, so there are no IMFs to overcome — you must break actual covalent bonds to melt them. Result: extremely high melting points.

Key Examples

  • Diamond (C): Each C bonded to 4 other C atoms in a continuous 3D network → hardest natural substance, MP ≈ 3550°C
  • Quartz / Silicon dioxide (SiO₂): Si–O–Si network extends infinitely → very hard, MP ≈ 1710°C
  • Silicon carbide (SiC): Industrial abrasive, extremely hard

Network Covalent Properties

  • Extremely high melting & boiling point
  • Very hard and brittle
  • Do NOT conduct electricity (no free e⁻ or ions)
  • Insoluble in water
  • No separate molecules — the whole crystal IS the molecule
Regents Example Diamond has an extremely high melting point despite being a covalent (nonmetal) substance. Explain. → Diamond is a network covalent solid. Every carbon atom is covalently bonded to 4 neighbors in a 3D network — there are no separate molecules and no IMFs. Melting requires breaking thousands of strong C–C covalent bonds, requiring enormous energy.
⚠️ Regents Trap! Students see "covalent compound" and assume "low melting point." Network covalent solids are the exception — diamond and SiO₂ melt at thousands of degrees. The question will give you a high-MP covalent substance and ask you to explain it. The answer is always: network covalent solid / extended covalent network.

Why are metals malleable but ionic crystals brittle? In an ionic crystal, shifting a layer of ions forces like charges (+ next to + or − next to −) into contact → repulsion shatters the crystal. In a metal, the electron sea simply flows to surround the newly repositioned cations — no repulsion, no shattering. This is why you can bend a copper pipe but a salt crystal cracks.

Alloys are metallic mixtures — atoms of different metals (or nonmetals like carbon in steel) mixed into a single metallic structure. Alloys often have better properties than pure metals: steel is stronger than pure iron, bronze is harder than pure copper. All alloys still have metallic bonding and a delocalized electron sea.

Graphite — the weird case: Graphite is also pure carbon like diamond, but is a soft conductor! In graphite, each C forms 3 bonds in flat hexagonal layers, with one electron per C delocalized between layers. Those delocalized electrons carry charge (conducts!) while the weak forces between layers allow them to slide (soft, makes marks on paper). Same element, totally different structure, totally different properties — a beautiful example of how bonding type determines everything.

⚡

Copper wiring: Copper's free electron sea is why nearly every electrical wire in your home is copper. Electrons zip through the entire metal as a single delocalized cloud — zero energy wasted forcing charge carriers to hop from atom to atom.

💎

Diamond tools: Industrial diamond-tipped drill bits and cutting blades exploit the network covalent structure of diamond — every C–C bond in the entire crystal must be broken to scratch it. Nothing harder exists in nature.

🏗️

Steel: Steel is an alloy of iron + carbon (typically 0.1–2%). The carbon atoms fit into the iron lattice and interfere with dislocation movement, making steel much harder and stronger than pure iron — the foundation of modern construction.

🪟

Glass (SiO₂): Window glass is based on silicon dioxide — a network covalent solid. Its three-dimensional Si–O–Si network gives it hardness and a very high melting point (it's made by melting quartz sand at ~1700°C).

Common Student Misconceptions

🔬 Misconception Busters — Unit 4: Chemical Bonding

❌ "Ionic compounds always conduct electricity"

Students omit the critical condition. Ionic compounds conduct ONLY when ions are mobile — dissolved in water (aqueous) or melted. Solid ionic compounds have ions locked in the lattice = no conductivity. This distinction is tested on almost every Regents exam.

✅ "Ionic compounds conduct electricity in aqueous solution or when melted, but NOT as solids."

❌ "Covalent bonds are weak"

Students confuse intramolecular covalent bonds (strong! C–C, C=O, N≡N) with intermolecular forces between molecules (weaker). The C–C bond in diamond is one of the strongest bonds in nature. The confusion arises because covalent COMPOUNDS often melt at low temperatures due to weak IMFs between molecules.

✅ "Covalent bonds within a molecule are strong. The forces BETWEEN separate covalent molecules (IMFs) are weaker than ionic lattice forces, so covalent compounds often have lower melting points."

❌ "Polar bonds = polar molecule"

CO₂ has two polar C=O bonds yet is a nonpolar molecule because the linear shape makes the dipoles cancel. CCl₄ has four polar C–Cl bonds yet is nonpolar due to tetrahedral symmetry. Students must consider BOTH bond polarity AND molecular shape/geometry.

✅ "A polar molecule requires polar bonds AND an asymmetric shape so the bond dipoles do NOT cancel. Always consider both factors."

❌ "Hydrogen bonding = any bond involving H"

Students think any molecule with hydrogen has hydrogen bonds as its IMF. True hydrogen bonding (as an IMF) requires H bonded specifically to F, O, or N. These atoms are uniquely small and electronegative, creating a strong enough partial charge to attract lone pairs on neighboring molecules. H bonded to C, Cl, S, etc. does NOT create true H-bonds.

✅ "Hydrogen bonding (as an IMF) = H covalently bonded to F, O, or N only. The 'FON' rule."

❌ "1.7 is an exact ionic/covalent boundary"

Students treat 1.7 as a hard line. HF has END ≈ 1.9 (above 1.7) but behaves covalently. Bond character is a spectrum. For Regents purposes, use 1.7 exactly as the cutoff, but understand it is an approximation for a continuous reality.

✅ "1.7 is the Regents standard cutoff. Bond character is actually a spectrum — use 1.7 for all exam purposes."

❌ "Covalent compounds always have low melting points"

Students learn covalent = low MP and apply it universally. Network covalent solids like diamond (MP ≈ 3550°C) and quartz/SiO₂ (MP ≈ 1710°C) have some of the highest melting points of any substance. There are no separate molecules — the entire crystal is one giant covalently bonded structure, so there are no IMFs to overcome; actual covalent bonds must be broken.

✅ "Most covalent molecular compounds have low MPs due to weak IMFs. Network covalent solids (diamond, SiO₂) are exceptions — their 3D covalent networks give them extremely high MPs."

❌ "Only ionic compounds conduct electricity"

Students forget that metals are a third category. Metals conduct electricity as solids because of their delocalized electron sea — electrons are always free to move, with no need to dissolve or melt. When a Regents question asks which substance conducts as a solid, the answer is always a metal.

✅ "Metals conduct electricity as solids (electron sea). Ionic compounds conduct only when dissolved or melted. Covalent compounds never conduct."

Bond Types

  • Ionic: Metal + Nonmetal → electron TRANSFER
  • Covalent: Nonmetal + Nonmetal → electron SHARING
  • Cation (+) = metal lost e⁻ · Anion (−) = nonmetal gained e⁻

END Scale

END = |EN₁ − EN₂|

  • 0.0–0.4 → Nonpolar Covalent
  • 0.5–1.7 → Polar Covalent
  • >1.7 → Ionic

Naming Ionic

  • Metal name + nonmetal root + -ide
  • Transition metals: use Roman numerals
  • FeCl₃ → iron(III) chloride
  • Polyatomic ions keep their names

Naming Covalent

  • Greek prefixes: mono, di, tri, tetra, penta...
  • "mono-" NOT on first element
  • CO₂ → carbon dioxide
  • N₂O₅ → dinitrogen pentoxide

Lewis Dot Rules

  • Group # = valence e⁻ = # dots
  • Place 1 per side, then pair up
  • Unpaired dots = # bonds formed
  • Ion: adjust dot count by charge

IMF Strength

  • LDF: all molecules, weakest
  • Dipole-dipole: polar molecules only
  • H-bonding: H bonded to F, O, or N
  • Stronger IMF → higher BP/MP

Metallic & Network Covalent

  • Metallic: Metal+Metal → electron sea
  • Conducts as solid · malleable · lustrous
  • Network covalent: 3D covalent network
  • Diamond & SiO₂ → extremely high MP
